## Summary

Yggdrasil, the World Tree, holds the nine worlds in its branches and roots. But unlike the eternal pillars of other mythologies, Yggdrasil is dying. A dragon gnaws at its roots. Four deer strip its leaves. A squirrel carries insults between them. And only the daily work of three women—the Norns—keeps the tree alive by pouring water and packing healing clay into its wounds.

The Norse cosmos does not sustain itself. It requires constant maintenance.

## Key Concepts

**Yggdrasil** (IG-drah-sil): "Odin's horse"—the World Tree. Named for a gallows, the tree holds all nine worlds. It is under constant attack and survives only through the work of the Norns.

**The Norns** — Urðr (URTH), Verðandi (VEHR-than-dee), Skuld (SKUUL-d): "What Was," "What Is Becoming," and "What Shall Be." The three women who tend Yggdrasil and carve the fates of all beings.

**Níðhöggr** (NEETH-hug-r): "Malice Striker." The dragon that gnaws at the root of Yggdrasil extending into Niflheimr. Represents entropy and dissolution.

**Ratatoskr** (RAT-ah-tosk-r): "Drill-tooth." A squirrel who runs up and down Yggdrasil's trunk carrying insults between the eagle above and the dragon below, escalating their conflict.

## The Three Wells

**Well of Urðr** (URTH-ar-broon-r): Beneath the root reaching to Ásgarðr. The well of fate. The Norns carve all destinies here.

**Mímir's Well** (MEE-mis-broon-r): Beneath the root reaching into Jötunheimr. The well of wisdom and memory. Odin traded his eye for a drink.

**Hvergelmir** (KWER-ghel-meer): Beneath the root reaching into Niflheimr. The ancient, primordial spring where Níðhöggr dwells.

## What Listeners Will Learn

The World Tree reveals the Norse understanding of reality: nothing is permanent. Everything requires work to maintain. The cosmos is not a monument—it's a structure held up by vigilance and daily labor. The Norns are not all-powerful creators; they are janitors with a bucket of water.

This episode establishes the core tension of Norse mythology: the gods know the end is coming, and they tend the tree anyway.
